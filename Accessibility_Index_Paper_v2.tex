\begin{abstract}
Choosing where to open a new grocery store is a spatial decision that
depends on at least three factors simultaneously: the demand that can
be captured from the surrounding population, how easily that population
can reach the candidate site through the road network, and how many
competing stores are already serving the same area. In practice, most
site screening tools treat these factors in isolation or rely on
informal rules of thumb, which leads to inconsistent rankings across
candidate sites and across different store brands.

This paper proposes a composite spatial accessibility index that
combines a gravity-based demand model, a road-network travel-time
score, and a competition density penalty into a single normalised
score for every candidate site in a study area. The model parameters
are calibrated separately for each brand using leave-one-out
cross-validation, which avoids the circular reasoning that arises when
known store locations are used both to train and to evaluate the same
model.

We apply the framework to two grocery chains operating in Mecklenburg
County, North Carolina: Harris Teeter, a premium chain, and Food Lion,
a value chain. Both calibrated models place known store locations
significantly above random chance and outperform simpler baselines
built on population density alone or competitor counts alone. The
calibrated weights recover a clear strategic difference between the
two chains without any prior assumption about their behaviour. The
paper closes with ranked top-ten candidate sites for each brand and
a discussion of how the framework can be extended to other retail
contexts.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Retail location decisions have been studied for more than half a
century, yet most operational tools used by site selection teams still
rely on demographic heat maps, fixed-radius drive-time buffers, or
internal scoring spreadsheets whose weights are set by judgement rather
than by data. Each of these shortcuts ignores at least one of the
three factors that determine whether a new store will succeed: the
amount of accessible demand, the ease of reaching the site through
the road network, and the level of competition already present in the
trade area.

A grocery chain choosing between two candidate parcels in the same
suburb needs to know all three things at once. If demand is high but
five competitors are already within two miles, the site is
unattractive. If demand is moderate but no other store of the same
type exists within five road miles, the site may capture a much larger
share of the local market. If accessibility is poor because the only
road into the parcel is a congested two-lane arterial, even abundant
demand will not translate into footfall. None of these judgements can
be made from a single layer of GIS data. They require a combined
score that respects the trade-offs.

This paper develops such a score. We define a composite spatial
accessibility index built from three components. The first component
is a Huff gravity demand potential, which estimates the share of
nearby population that a candidate site would capture given a set of
existing competitors. The second component is a road-network
accessibility score derived from shortest-path travel times computed
on the OpenStreetMap road graph using a multi-source Dijkstra search.
The third component is a competition density penalty, computed as the
number of competing stores per ten thousand residents within the
catchment radius. The three components are normalised onto a common
scale and combined into a single score using brand-specific weights.

The weights are not assumed. We calibrate them for each brand using
leave-one-out cross-validation over an iterative grid search on
known store locations. For every candidate parameter combination,
each known store is held out in turn, the remaining stores are used
as ground truth, and the held-out store's percentile rank among all
candidate sites is recorded. The mean rank across all held-out
stores is the calibration objective. This procedure prevents the
model from rewarding itself for ranking a store highly when that
store also appears in its own competitor set.

We apply the framework to Mecklenburg County, North Carolina, and
calibrate it separately for two grocery chains with markedly
different market positions. Harris Teeter is a premium chain that
competes on store experience and tends to follow population demand.
Food Lion is a value chain with broader geographic coverage and a
stronger incentive to find underserved gaps in the competitive map.
A model that is doing what it claims to do should recover this
strategic contrast from the data, and it does.

The paper makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A composite scoring framework that integrates demand,
      accessibility, and competition into a single brand-specific
      index, with explicit normalisation and weight calibration steps.
\item A leave-one-out cross-validation pipeline that calibrates the
      framework for any chain with at least twenty store locations
      in the study area, with a permutation test and three baseline
      comparisons that establish statistical significance.
\item A multi-source Dijkstra implementation that computes the full
      block-group-to-network-node distance matrix in a single graph
      traversal, reducing routing pre-computation time by roughly a
      factor of five compared with sequential single-source
      Dijkstra on the same hardware.
\item Ranked top-ten candidate sites for Harris Teeter and Food
      Lion in Mecklenburg County, with a discussion of how the
      contrasting calibrated weights translate into different
      spatial patterns of recommendation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

The framework developed in this paper draws on four streams of prior
work: gravity models of retail demand, spatial accessibility measures,
competitive facility location, and shortest-path computation on road
networks.

\subsection{Gravity models of retail demand}

The Huff probabilistic trading area model \cite{huff1964} remains the
dominant formulation in retail demand estimation. Huff modelled the
probability that a consumer at origin $i$ patronises store $j$ as the
ratio of that store's attractiveness divided by distance raised to a
decay exponent, against the same quantity summed over all alternatives.
Nakanishi and Cooper \cite{nakanishi1974} reformulated the Huff model
as a multiplicative competitive interaction model and showed how its
parameters can be estimated by least squares on observed market
shares. Fotheringham \cite{fotheringham1988} extended the framework
to recognise that consumers do not consider every store in a region,
only a smaller choice set defined by perception and habit. More
recent retail location studies use revealed preference methods.
Ellickson, Houghton, and Timmins \cite{ellickson2012} estimated
network economies in United States grocery chains and found that
chains cluster near existing stores up to a competitive threshold,
beyond which the marginal store cannibalises rather than reinforces
demand.

\subsection{Spatial accessibility measures}

Hansen \cite{hansen1959} introduced spatial accessibility as a
gravity potential, defining the accessibility of a location as the
sum of opportunities at all reachable destinations weighted by an
impedance function of distance. Geurs and van Wee \cite{geurs2004}
review the four principal classes of accessibility measures
(infrastructure-based, location-based, person-based, and
utility-based) and argue that location-based gravity measures remain
the most useful for infrastructure and land-use planning. Handy and
Niemeier \cite{handy1997} contrast cumulative-opportunity measures,
which count destinations within a fixed cutoff, against potential
measures, which weight destinations continuously by distance. The
formulation used in this paper follows the potential measure
tradition. Luo and Wang \cite{luo2003} developed the two-step floating
catchment area method to measure spatial access to health care
facilities, an approach that has since been adapted to grocery store
access in food desert studies. Apparicio, Cloutier, and Shearmur
\cite{apparicio2007} applied accessibility measures to evaluate
supermarket access in Montreal and showed that conclusions about
underserved neighbourhoods are sensitive to the choice of impedance
function.

\subsection{Competitive facility location}

Hotelling's stability in competition result \cite{hotelling1929}
established the basic intuition that competing firms tend to locate
near one another to capture the largest share of a linear market, and
this insight has motivated decades of work on competitive facility
location. Berman and Krass \cite{berman2002} formalised the
generalised maximal covering location problem, which selects facility
sites to maximise covered demand subject to capacity constraints.
Datta and Sudhir \cite{datta2013} estimated how zoning regulations
affect retail format differentiation, finding that policies which
reduce spatial separation increase product differentiation between
competing chains. ReVelle and Eiselt \cite{revelle2005} survey
forty years of facility location research and identify calibrated
competitive models as the most practically relevant class for
retail chains operating in saturated markets. The composite index
proposed here belongs to that class.

\subsection{Shortest-path computation on road networks}

Dijkstra's algorithm \cite{dijkstra1959} is the standard method for
computing single-source shortest paths on a non-negatively weighted
graph. For a road network with $V$ nodes and $E$ edges, the algorithm
runs in $O((V + E)\log V)$ time using a binary heap. When shortest
paths are needed from many sources, the naive approach is to call
single-source Dijkstra once per source, with total cost $O(S(V+E)\log
V)$ for $S$ sources. Multi-source Dijkstra reduces this by running
a single graph traversal from all sources simultaneously, which is
particularly efficient when the source set is dense relative to the
graph size.

OpenStreetMap \cite{haklay2008} provides freely available road network
data of comparable accuracy to commercial sources for most urban
regions. Boeing \cite{boeing2017} demonstrates that the OSMnx library
enables reproducible acquisition and analysis of OpenStreetMap street
networks.

\subsection{Position of this work}

The framework proposed here differs from prior work in three
respects. First, it integrates demand, accessibility, and competition
into a single composite score rather than treating them as separate
analyses. Second, the relative weights of the three components are
not fixed at the analyst's discretion but are calibrated separately
for each brand using leave-one-out cross-validation, which makes the
calibrated weights themselves a meaningful output of the model.
Third, the routing layer is implemented as a single multi-source
Dijkstra call on a precomputed sparse graph, which makes the full
calibration grid search tractable on a single workstation without
the infrastructure overhead of a production routing engine.

\section{Study Area and Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Study area}

The study area is Mecklenburg County, North Carolina, which contains
the city of Charlotte and surrounding municipalities such as Matthews,
Huntersville, and Pineville. According to the 2020 Decennial Census,
the county has a resident population of approximately 1.1 million
distributed across 559 census block groups. Mecklenburg County is
representative of a large United States metropolitan area in terms
of road network size, demographic mix, and competitive grocery
landscape, making it a suitable test bed for the proposed framework.

\subsection{Road network}

The road network was downloaded from OpenStreetMap using the OSMnx
Python library. The extracted graph contains 282,751 nodes and
585,666 edges. Edge weights are travel times in seconds computed
from posted speed limits. For shortest-path computation, the graph is
stored as a Compressed Sparse Row (CSR) matrix, required by the SciPy
sparse graph routines used for Dijkstra computation.

\subsection{Demographic data}

Population counts and median household income for each of the 559
block groups were obtained from the 2018--2022 American Community
Survey five-year estimates. The geometric centroid of each block group
polygon is used as the demand point in all Huff and accessibility
calculations.

\subsection{Store locations and competitor set}

Harris Teeter and Food Lion store locations were obtained from brand
store-finder pages in January 2024 and cross-referenced with
OpenStreetMap. Of 29 Harris Teeter stores and 27 Food Lion stores in
Mecklenburg County, 28 and 26 respectively were successfully snapped
to the nearest road network node within 500 metres. The single
unmatched store for each brand is excluded from the calibration set.
The competitor set for each brand consists of all other grocery stores
in the county, covering formats from premium to discount.

\subsection{Candidate sites}

Candidate sites are derived from OpenStreetMap commercial land use
polygons filtered to areas between 500 and 50,000 square metres.
After deduplication and removal of polygons overlapping existing
stores, 2,450 distinct candidate centroids remain.

\subsection{Notation}

Let $j$ index the candidate sites and $i$ index the census block
groups. Let $\mathrm{pop}_i$ denote the population of block group $i$,
$r$ the catchment radius in miles, $\mathcal{B}_j(r)$ the set of block
groups within $r$ miles of candidate $j$, $d_{ij}$ the Euclidean
distance from block group $i$ to candidate $j$, $t_{ij}$ the
shortest-path travel time on the road network, and $\mathcal{C}_j(K)$
the set of $K$ nearest competitor stores to candidate $j$.

\subsection{Huff demand potential}
\label{sec:huff}

\begin{equation}
P_j = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_j(r)}
      \mathrm{pop}_i \cdot
      \frac{d_{ij}^{-\beta}}
           {d_{ij}^{-\beta} +
            \sum_{k \in \mathcal{C}_j(K)} d_{ik}^{-\beta}}
\label{eq:huff}
\end{equation}

where $\beta > 0$ is the distance decay exponent. The ratio is the
share of block group $i$'s demand captured by candidate $j$ given its
$K$ nearest competitors. Following Huff's original formulation,
Euclidean distance is used because consumer perception of effort to
a shopping destination is closer to a straight-line judgement than
to a precise routing computation.

\subsection{Road accessibility}
\label{sec:access}

\begin{equation}
R_j = \frac{\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_j(r)} \mathrm{pop}_i}
           {\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_j(r)} \mathrm{pop}_i \cdot t_{ij}}
\label{eq:access}
\end{equation}

$R_j$ is the reciprocal of the population-weighted mean road travel
time to candidate $j$. Sites with shorter mean travel time to larger
populations receive higher $R_j$, consistent with the Hansen
\cite{hansen1959} potential accessibility tradition.

\subsection{Competition penalty}
\label{sec:comp}

\begin{equation}
S_j = \frac{N_{\mathrm{comp},j}}
           {\mathrm{pop}_{j,r} / 10\,000}
\label{eq:comp}
\end{equation}

where $N_{\mathrm{comp},j}$ is the competitor count within $r$ miles
and $\mathrm{pop}_{j,r}$ is the catchment population. High $S_j$
indicates a saturated trade area.

\subsection{Normalisation}
\label{sec:norm}

Each component is mapped to $[0.01, 1.0]$ using percentile-robust
linear scaling:

\begin{equation}
\hat{x}_j = 0.01 + 0.99 \cdot
            \frac{\mathrm{clip}(x_j, q_1, q_{99}) - q_1}
                 {q_{99} - q_1}
\label{eq:norm}
\end{equation}

where $q_1$ and $q_{99}$ are the 1st and 99th percentiles of $x$
across all candidates. Clipping prevents extreme outliers from
compressing the rest of the distribution.

\subsection{Composite score}
\label{sec:composite}

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Score}_j =
W_1 \hat{P}_j + W_2 \hat{R}_j - W_3 \hat{S}_j
\label{eq:composite}
\end{equation}

subject to $W_1 + W_2 + W_3 = 1$ and $W_i \geq 0$. Demand and
accessibility contribute positively; competition enters as a penalty.

\subsection{Multi-source Dijkstra routing}
\label{sec:routing}

Computing $t_{ij}$ for all 559 block groups via sequential
single-source Dijkstra requires 559 independent graph traversals.
Because the road graph is undirected, a single multi-source Dijkstra
call from all block group nodes simultaneously produces the full
$559 \times 282{,}751$ distance matrix in one pass using
\texttt{scipy.sparse.csgraph.dijkstra} with \texttt{directed=False}.
The matrix is cached on disk; subsequent calibration runs that
change $r$, $\beta$, $K$, $W_1$, $W_2$, or $W_3$ reuse the cache
and complete in under half a second each.

\section{Hyperparameter Calibration}
\label{sec:calib}

\subsection{Leave-one-out cross-validation}

For each parameter vector $\theta = (r, \beta, K, W_1, W_2, W_3)$
and each known store $s$, we: (1) remove $s$ from the ground truth
and competitor set, (2) score all 2,450 candidates, and (3) record
the percentile rank of the held-out location. The LOOCV score is
the mean percentile rank across all held-out stores.

\subsection{Iterative grid search}

We use a four-stage search. Stage A evaluates a coarse Huff parameter
grid and selects the top-20 combinations by fast non-cross-validated
rank. Stage B runs full LOOCV on those 20 and selects the best Huff
configuration. Stage C evaluates a weight grid $(W_1, W_2)$ with
$W_3 = 1 - W_1 - W_2$ at the Stage B configuration. Stage D runs
full LOOCV on the top-10 weight combinations. If the winner lands on
a grid boundary, the grid extends and the search repeats. Food Lion
converged in three iterations; Harris Teeter required six.

\subsection{Strategic interpretation}

Harris Teeter's $W_1 = 0.75$ places three quarters of the composite
score on demand potential. Combined with the smaller catchment radius
and higher decay exponent, the model implies a preference for dense,
concentrated demand. Food Lion's $W_3 = 0.85$ places 85\% of the
score on competition avoidance, with a wider catchment and lower
decay implying a broader, more dispersed trade area. Both patterns
emerge purely from observed store locations with no prior assumption
about either chain's strategy.

\section{Validation}
\label{sec:valid}

\subsection{Permutation test}

We score all 2,450 candidates under the calibrated parameters,
compute the mean percentile rank $\bar{p}$ of matched stores, then
draw 10,000 random subsets of the same size from the candidate pool
and record their mean ranks. The $p$-value is the fraction of random
subsets meeting or exceeding $\bar{p}$.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The weight contrast is the principal finding}

The calibrated weight vectors are the most useful output of the
framework. Harris Teeter and Food Lion operate in the same county with
the same road conditions, yet their weights are nearly mirror images.
A single-parameter model would systematically misrank candidates for
both chains, for opposite reasons. Brand-specific calibration is
essential.

\subsection{Why population density alone fails}

Population density scores below random for both brands. Both chains
sit in suburban and exurban locations rather than the dense urban
core. They access population indirectly through road connectivity and
competitive spacing, not by co-locating with the highest raw density.

\subsection{Why the road accessibility weight is small}

$W_2 = 0.05$ for both chains reflects the relatively uniform road
connectivity within Mecklenburg County. In a study area bisected by
major barriers, the calibration procedure would be expected to
assign a larger share to $W_2$.

\subsection{Limitations}

The competitive landscape is treated as static at the calibration
date. The framework requires at least 20 stores per chain for reliable
LOOCV and permutation tests. Calibrated parameters are specific to
Mecklenburg County and should not be transferred to other metros
without recalibration. The framework does not include real estate
cost, zoning, or labour market conditions.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper proposed a composite spatial accessibility index for
grocery store site selection combining a Huff demand potential, a
road network accessibility score, and a competition density penalty
under brand-specific calibrated weights. Applied to Harris Teeter
and Food Lion in Mecklenburg County, the LOOCV calibration recovered
clearly different parameter vectors: $W_1 = 0.75$ for Harris Teeter
(demand first) and $W_3 = 0.85$ for Food Lion (competition
avoidance). A permutation test rejected the null hypothesis of chance
rankings at $p < 0.01$ for both brands. The composite model
outperformed random selection, population density, and inverse
competitor count for both brands.

The multi-source Dijkstra pre-computation (44.3\,s) reduced the
routing bottleneck by a factor of 4.7 compared with sequential
Dijkstra, making the multi-stage calibration grid search tractable
on a single workstation.
